\chapter{Discussion}%
\label{chap:discussion}

The preceding three empirical chapters addressed the research questions of this thesis in sequence: who is targeted in anti-Christian online hate speech (Chapter~\ref{chap:who}), how that hostility is rhetorically expressed (Chapter~\ref{chap:how}), and what moral motivations underlie it across language communities (Chapter~\ref{chap:why}). This chapter synthesises those findings into a coherent cross-lingual account, situates them in relation to the theoretical frameworks and prior work introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:related-work}, examines the socio-political contexts that may explain the patterns observed, critically evaluates the analytical pipeline developed in Chapter~\ref{chap:methods}, and outlines directions for future research.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Synthesis of Key Findings}
\label{sec:discussion-synthesis}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The central question of this thesis was whether anti-Christian hate speech can be decomposed into an interpretable, cross-linguistically comparable structure of targets, rhetorical forms, and moral motivations. The short answer is yes --- but the structure that emerges is not uniform across languages. Rather, it reveals three markedly different modes of hostility, each anchored in a distinct social logic. This section summarises the main findings at a level of abstraction above the numerical details reported in Chapters~\ref{chap:who}--\ref{chap:why}, and identifies where the three language communities converge and where they diverge.

\textbf{Answering RQ1: Who is attacked.}
All three communities share a transnational core of religiously-grounded targeting: Jesus, Catholic identity labels, and the institutional Church appear as objects of hostility across Chinese, English, and Japanese alike. A shared cognitive schema --- the \textit{brainwashing} metaphor (EN: \textit{brainwashed}; ZH: 洗脑; JA: 洗脳) --- further attests to cross-linguistic commonalities. Beyond this shared core, however, the target constellations diverge sharply. English-language targeting tends to be broad, distributing attention across theological figures, clergy, collective identity labels, and contemporary political actors. Chinese targeting concentrates on the clerical hierarchy and institutional structures of the Church, framed through a register of deception and exploitation. Japanese targeting is almost entirely absorbed by a single politically-situated narrative centred on the Unification Church, its political patron, and the party that enabled both. These divergences suggest that the ``who'' of hate speech is not a fixed attribute of the religion itself, but is shaped by the institutional and political salience of Christianity in each setting.

\textbf{Answering RQ2: How hatred is expressed.}
The analysis of attack frames revealed a striking regularity: frame selection appears to be systematically shaped by the social category of the target rather than by language alone. Across all three communities, clerical role-targets attract allegations of sexual abuse; collective identity labels attract general rhetorical attack and moral accusation; and the Japanese Unification Church--LDP target pair attracts political interference and economic exploitation frames. This target-to-frame alignment suggests that hate speech operates according to genre-like conventions, in which attackers select the rhetorical weapon appropriate to the target's perceived social role. At the language level, each community also exhibits a distinctive frame emphasis: English discourse relies predominantly on sarcasm and broad moral condemnation; Chinese discourse foregrounds institutional corruption and abuse cover-ups; Japanese discourse is structured around political and financial predation.

\textbf{Answering RQ3: Why people hate.}
The moral motivation analysis revealed the most consequential cross-lingual divergence. English-language discourse is negatively aligned across all five classical Moral Foundations axes simultaneously, constituting a broad moral condemnation. Chinese and Japanese discourse, by contrast, remain relatively normative on the classical foundations but shift sharply negative on the Symbolic Threat and Realistic Threat axes. This pattern suggests that the English community tends to indict Christianity for moral failure within a shared ethical framework, whereas the East Asian communities tend to frame it as a danger to cultural identity and social cohesion --- a fundamentally different rhetorical logic with distinct implications for interpretation and moderation.

\textbf{An unexpected structural regularity.}
One finding that was not anticipated at the outset deserves explicit attention: the high empirical correlation among the five classical Moral Foundations axes within this corpus (pairwise $r \approx 0.8$). In survey-based MFT research, the five foundations are theoretically and empirically distinguishable \citep{graham2009liberals}. Their near-collapse into a single dimension in this hate speech corpus may indicate that online hate speech activates moral cognition in a qualitatively different mode from everyday moral reasoning --- one in which the entire moral repertoire is deployed simultaneously as a ``condemnation bundle'' rather than selectively. This possibility has implications for both theory and method, and is discussed further in Section~\ref{sec:discussion-theory}.

\section{Theoretical Contributions: Dialogue with Prior Work}
\label{sec:discussion-theory}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

This section situates the findings within the theoretical landscape reviewed in Chapter~\ref{chap:related-work}. Following the ``discussion cycle'' approach, each contribution is grounded in a specific finding, compared with relevant prior work, and evaluated for its broader implications.

\subsection{Beyond Detection: The Internal Structure of Hateful Discourse}

\citet{matamoros-fernandez_racism_2021} argued that the dominant detection paradigm in computational hate speech research strips away the social power dynamics, historical oppression, and platform ideology that give hateful discourse its force. The present findings lend empirical support to this critique. The who-how-why pipeline demonstrates that hate speech possesses a three-layer internal structure --- target, rhetorical form, and moral motivation --- that a binary detection approach cannot recover. In particular, the finding that attack frame selection is determined by target category (Section~\ref{sec:rq2-by-target}) suggests that hateful discourse is produced according to genre-like regularities rather than as an undifferentiated expression of hostility.

This structural finding also extends \citet{ollagnier_unsupervised_2023}'s unsupervised clustering approach. While their pipeline successfully identified topical structures within hate speech corpora, it stopped short of providing an interpretive account of the rhetoric and moral logic within those clusters. The present thesis builds on the clustering scaffold to show that computational grouping and structured sociological interpretation can be integrated rather than treated as separate tasks. That said, it should be acknowledged that the integration is partial: the who-how-why layers are analysed sequentially rather than jointly, and their mutual interactions remain underexplored.

\subsection{Intergroup Threat Theory Extended to Religious Symbols}

\citet{stephan2000integrated} formulated Intergroup Threat Theory as a model of hostility driven by perceived threats to material resources (Realistic Threat) or cultural values (Symbolic Threat), primarily in racial, ethnic, and immigration contexts. The present findings appear to extend this framework to religious hate speech in East Asia, where Christianity functions as a symbolically foreign institution.

The RQ3 results are consistent with ITT's core predictions. In the Japanese case, the Unification Church is attacked not on theological grounds but as an organisation that has materially penetrated domestic political processes --- a pattern that maps onto Realistic Threat. The explicit invocation of the Church's Korean origins (韓国 appearing in the Japanese top-20 targets) further amplifies the outsider framing. In the Chinese case, the dominant Symbolic Threat signature --- discourse that frames Christianity as culturally incompatible rather than simply morally objectionable --- aligns with ITT's prediction that threat-based hostility is activated when the outgroup is perceived as a civilisational outsider. Importantly, neither of these patterns obtains in English, where the Realistic Threat axis is the only positive one, and Christianity is attacked as an institution that has failed morally from within. This contrast is consistent with \citet{Yancey2014}'s observation that anti-Christian bias in Western settings operates through a different mechanism --- resistance to majority cultural power --- rather than through the threat-to-ingroup logic that ITT describes.

However, extending ITT to this domain also exposes a limitation: ITT was developed to model intergroup attitudes at the individual psychological level, whereas the present study analyses discourse patterns at the corpus level. The alignment between discourse-level FrameAxis projections and ITT's theoretical predictions is suggestive but does not constitute a direct test of the theory's psychological mechanisms. Future work combining corpus analysis with survey-based threat perception measures could strengthen this connection.

\subsection{Moral Foundations Theory: Cross-Lingual Activation Patterns and the Condemnation Bundle}

Moral Foundations Theory \citep{graham2009liberals, haidt2012righteous} proposes that moral intuitions are universally organised along five dimensions. The present findings do not challenge this universality, but they do reveal an unexpected pattern that complicates its application to hate speech. In English, all five foundations are simultaneously negative, forming a ``condemnation bundle'' rather than the differentiated moral profiles that characterise political ideology in \citet{graham2009liberals}'s original survey data. In that work, liberals and conservatives were shown to differ systematically in which foundations they prioritise; in this hate speech corpus, the five foundations appear to collapse into a single aggressive dimension.

This may suggest that online hate speech activates a qualitatively different mode of moral cognition. One possible explanation is that hate speech, as a communicative genre, favours maximal moral delegitimation: the rhetorical goal is not nuanced moral evaluation but comprehensive condemnation, which is best served by invoking all available moral charges simultaneously. Another possibility is that the FrameAxis method, which relies on dictionary-based cosine projections, may lack the sensitivity to distinguish foundations that are activated through similar vocabulary in online registers. These two explanations are not mutually exclusive, and disentangling them would require either a more granular lexicon or a complementary annotation-based approach.

For the East Asian communities, the contrast is illuminating. The classical foundations remain relatively positive while the Threat axes turn sharply negative, producing what might be described as an \emph{exclusionary framing}: the moral charge is not that Christianity has violated shared ethical standards but that it is culturally incompatible with the ingroup. This is distinct from the \emph{immanent critique} pattern observed in English, where the attackers invoke a common moral vocabulary and argue that the religion has betrayed it. These two rhetorical strategies are likely to require different interpretive and moderation responses.

\subsection{Implicit Hate Speech in a Multilingual Setting}

\citet{elsherief_latent_2021} demonstrated that a substantial proportion of English-language hate speech operates through implicit framing --- coded language, narrative insinuation, and indirect accusation --- rather than through explicit slurs. The present findings extend this observation to a multilingual religious context. The Sexual Abuse Frame in Chinese (realised through institutional-corruption vocabulary rather than slurs) and the Political Interference frame in Japanese (realised through political vocabulary applied to a religious target) are both prototypical instances of latent hate: they carry hostile force through narrative allegation rather than lexical markers.

What the present study adds is a cross-lingual dimension that \citet{elsherief_latent_2021}'s English-only analysis could not address. Specifically, the finding that Dehumanisation vocabulary is sparse in English but more developed in Chinese (毒瘤, \textit{malignant tumour}) and Japanese (ゴキブリ, \textit{cockroach}) complicates the assumption, implicit in much of the detection literature, that explicit dehumanisation is the primary severity marker across cultural contexts. This suggests that severity assessment in multilingual moderation cannot simply transfer lexical severity scales from English to other languages.

\section{Socio-Cultural Contextualisation}
\label{sec:discussion-context}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The divergent target profiles, frame repertoires, and moral motivation signatures described above do not emerge in a vacuum. They reflect specific contemporary social and political circumstances. This section examines the contextual factors that may explain the observed patterns and, where possible, proposes mechanisms by which macro-level events are translated into micro-level discourse features.

\subsection{Japan: The Political Channelling of Religious Hostility}

The near-total dominance of Topic~0 in the Japanese corpus is a direct reflection of the most consequential political event in recent Japanese history. The assassination of former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe in July 2022 triggered an extended public reckoning with the Liberal Democratic Party's decades-long relationship with the Family Federation for World Peace and Unification \citep{mullins2021religion}. This relationship had remained largely hidden from public scrutiny; its sudden exposure created a ``revelatory moment'' in which accumulated public frustration with the conservative establishment found a concrete institutional target.

The mechanism by which this political event was translated into anti-religious discourse appears to involve what political psychologists call \textit{displaced attribution}: the Unification Church functions as a symbolically available proxy through which citizens can express political grievances (corruption, foreign influence, democratic erosion) that would otherwise lack a clear institutional target. The religion is attacked not primarily for what it teaches but for what it \textit{enabled}: financial predation through 霊感商法 (spirit-based sales fraud), electoral manipulation through Church-organised canvassing networks, and the shielding of these practices from regulatory scrutiny. This channelling mechanism explains several otherwise puzzling features of the Japanese findings: the dominance of Political Interference and Economic Exploitation frames over theological critique, the appearance of 韓国 (South Korea) as a target encoding foreign origin, and the threat-based moral profile that frames the organisation as dangerous rather than merely wrong.

\subsection{China: Institutional Scandal as Civilisational Evidence}

The Chinese pattern operates through a different mechanism. The concentration of the Sexual Abuse Frame and Institutional Rot vocabulary on clerical targets is consistent with what might be called \textit{evidential amplification}: individual cases of clerical misconduct are taken up not merely as evidence of institutional failure but as confirmation of a pre-existing narrative about Western civilisational hypocrisy. In a digital culture characterised by secular pragmatism and scepticism toward Western moral authority, clerical abuse scandals serve a dual rhetorical function. First, they delegitimise specific religious institutions; second, and more consequentially, they validate a broader worldview in which Western cultural imports are systematically suspicious.

The distinctive pejorative 神棍 (\textit{religious fraudster/charlatan}) captures this register precisely: it frames clergy not as mistaken but as deliberately deceptive, a framing that resonates with the anti-institutional scepticism characteristic of Chinese internet culture. The Symbolic Threat signature in RQ3 adds a further layer: clerical scandals are transformed from evidence of \textit{particular} institutional failure into evidence of \textit{civilisational} incompatibility. This rhetorical move --- from misconduct to cultural non-belonging --- may reflect broader dynamics of Chinese internet nationalism in which Western cultural institutions are systematically positioned as threats to social cohesion \citep{yang2011religion}.

\subsection{English: Moral Indictment of an Insider Institution}

English anti-Christian discourse operates in a setting where Christianity remains culturally dominant, making hostility a form of resistance to majority power rather than suspicion of an outsider institution \citep{Yancey2014, scheitle2020}. The co-prominence of Trump alongside Jesus and Catholic institutional targets reflects the deeply politicised character of Anglophone religious discourse since 2016, in which Christian identity has been instrumentalised by right-wing populism in ways that generate sustained counter-mobilisation.

The mechanism here appears to be \textit{moral accountability}: because Christianity is perceived as holding cultural and political power, it is held to a correspondingly high moral standard, and its perceived failures --- complicity in abuse, resistance to gender equality, entanglement with authoritarian politics --- are framed as betrayals of that standard. This explains the broad negative profile across all five Moral Foundations axes: it is not one specific failure but the entire moral ecology of institutionalised Christianity that is being indicted. The relative positivity of the Realistic Threat axis is consistent with this interpretation: Christianity is experienced as a morally failed insider, not an existentially threatening outsider.

\section{Pipeline Evaluation: Affordances and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion-pipeline}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

A stated objective of this thesis is to develop a reusable analytical pipeline. Having reported the substantive findings, it is necessary to assess, with appropriate candour, both what the pipeline achieves and where its design introduces constraints on the conclusions that can be drawn.

\subsection{Affordances}

The modular architecture (BERTopic~$\to$~RQ1~$\to$~RQ2~$\to$~RQ3) offers several genuine strengths. Cross-lingual centroid alignment enabled multilingual topic formation without translation, a non-trivial achievement for three structurally distinct writing systems. The three-layer extraction designs for both RQ1 (NER~$\to$~gazetteer~$\to$~LLM fallback) and RQ2 (dependency parsing~$\to$~predicate window~$\to$~LLM classification) were specifically motivated by the zero-subject and topic-drop constructions that are grammatically normal in Chinese and Japanese, ensuring coverage of structurally incomplete sentences that simpler approaches would miss. The FrameAxis projection for RQ3 \citep{Kwak_2021} avoids the prohibitive cost of constructing labelled moral-motivation datasets across three languages while remaining grounded in established theoretical frameworks. Finally, the overall design is reusable: gazetteers, frame taxonomies, and prompts can be adapted to other hate speech targets through substitution rather than redesign.

\subsection{Limitations and Their Impact on Validity}

Several design choices constrain the scope and reliability of the findings. These are discussed in terms of their impact on the internal and external validity of the conclusions.

\textbf{Corpus construction bias.}
The keyword-based data collection strategy introduces systematic selection bias: only texts that employ explicit surface markers of anti-Christian content enter the corpus. Implicit hate speech that avoids triggering keywords --- irony, allusion, contextually coded language --- is excluded by construction. This means that the prevalence and variety of implicit hostility are likely underestimated. The frame and motivation profiles reported in Chapters~\ref{chap:how} and~\ref{chap:why} should therefore be understood as characterising the keyword-legible portion of anti-Christian discourse, not its full population. Additionally, models fine-tuned on back-translated data may introduce translation artefacts that affect the Chinese and Japanese sub-corpora in ways that are difficult to quantify post hoc.

\textbf{Clustering sensitivity.}
The 47-topic BERTopic solution depends on specific hyperparameter choices (UMAP: \texttt{n\_neighbors=30}; HDBSCAN: \texttt{min\_cluster\_size=20}). Topics at the margin of this configuration may split or merge under alternative settings, potentially affecting topic-level frame and motivation profiles. A systematic sensitivity analysis was not conducted and remains an open question for replication. Moreover, the noise label ($-1$) excludes documents that may contain analytically consequential borderline content.

\textbf{LLM non-determinism.}
The Gemini API calls used for target fallback (RQ1) and frame classification (RQ2) introduce non-determinism. The frame classification prompt was developed iteratively through pilot testing rather than validated against a formally constructed gold-standard annotation set. Estimated inter-rater agreement between the LLM and a human annotator on a random sample would strengthen confidence in the frame distributions; its absence constitutes a limitation. API quota constraints also prevented systematic multi-run variance estimation.

\textbf{FrameAxis epistemological constraints.}
The cross-sectional nature of the corpus means that the analysis captures the structural distribution of moral vocabularies but cannot track how specific events --- such as the Abe assassination or individual abuse scandals --- trigger moral framing shifts over time. What the pipeline measures is discourse structure, not event causality. Furthermore, dictionary coverage gaps (CMFD polarity inferred via negation rules; J-MFD developed for general text rather than online political discourse) may introduce bias in axis scores for topics whose hostility is expressed through neologisms or coded terms absent from the dictionaries. These constraints are inherent to the FrameAxis method as currently implemented, and should inform the interpretation of the moral motivation profiles accordingly.

Notwithstanding these limitations, the central empirical claims of the thesis --- that anti-Christian hate speech exhibits a three-layer structure, that this structure varies systematically across languages, and that the variation aligns with theoretically predicted patterns --- rest on convergent evidence across multiple analytical layers rather than on any single pipeline component. The limitations affect the precision of specific estimates (e.g., exact frame proportions) more than they undermine the structural patterns themselves.

\section{Practical Implications}
\label{sec:discussion-implications}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The findings of this thesis have direct implications for how online hate speech involving religious communities is detected and moderated in practice.

The most consistent practical lesson is that lexical or keyword-based content moderation is insufficient for the forms of anti-Christian hate speech most prevalent in this corpus. A system that flags explicit slurs would miss the bulk of the hostile content identified here: the Sexual Abuse Frame constructed through co-occurring institutional-corruption vocabulary, the Political Interference frame built around political vocabulary applied to a religious target, and the pervasive Rhetorical Attack frame that operates through sarcasm and critical invective. This is precisely the structural feature of implicit hate that \citet{elsherief_latent_2021} identified as the frontier challenge for automated moderation; the present study extends that diagnosis to a multilingual religious context.

The three language communities also require differentiated moderation strategies. In Japanese, the challenge is distinguishing legitimate political commentary on Church--state ties from hateful content targeting vulnerable believers. In Chinese, pattern-level detection of cover-up vocabulary co-occurring with clerical role labels is more reliable than any single keyword. In English, the breadth of the moral condemnation profile means that the primary challenge is distinguishing protected religious critique from targeted harassment --- a distinction that requires frame-level classification rather than sentiment scoring alone.

More broadly, the who-how-why framework suggests a reframing of the moderation task: from binary detection to \textit{structured diagnosis}. Rather than returning a single label, a moderation-oriented application of the pipeline could return a structured report specifying the target, the attack frame, and the moral motivation. This would support more precise human review, more informative appeals processes, and richer platform-level analytics. Such a reframing aligns with recent calls for more explainable moderation systems \citep{mathew_hatexplain_2021} and reflects the thesis's foundational commitment: developing an interpretive framework that makes the structure of hate speech legible to social analysis, and ultimately, to governance.

\section{Directions for Future Research}
\label{sec:discussion-future}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Several directions for future work follow directly from the limitations and contributions discussed above. Each addresses a specific gap in the current study and is designed to be actionable for subsequent researchers.

First, the cross-sectional constraint motivates a \textbf{longitudinal extension} of the pipeline. Collecting corpus data with high-resolution timestamps would enable interrupted time-series analysis, allowing researchers to measure how specific triggering events --- such as the Abe assassination or individual abuse scandal revelations --- shift the moral axis profiles of online discourse. This would close the explanatory gap between discourse structure (what the present study measures) and event causality (what it cannot directly address).

Second, the dictionary coverage limitations motivate the construction of \textbf{platform-specific, register-adapted moral lexicons} for Chinese and Japanese online discourse. Corpus-derived lexicons built from social media text, with polarity validated through human annotation, would replace the approximations introduced by applying general-language dictionaries and enable more reliable axis-level comparisons across individual MFT foundations.

Third, the frame classification step would benefit from a \textbf{formal inter-rater reliability study} comparing LLM-assigned labels with human annotations on a held-out sample, and from the development of a gold-standard benchmark dataset for attack frame classification in trilingual religious hate speech. Such a resource would enable calibrated confidence estimates for the frame distributions and support systematic comparison across pipeline variants.

Fourth, the high correlation among the five classical MFT axes --- the ``condemnation bundle'' discussed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion-theory} --- raises a theoretical question that warrants \textbf{dedicated investigation}: is this collapse an artefact of the FrameAxis method's dictionary granularity, or does it reflect a genuine feature of moral cognition in the hate speech genre? Combining corpus-level analysis with controlled experiments or survey measures could help disentangle these possibilities.

Finally, the modularity of the who-how-why framework invites \textbf{domain transfer}. Replacing the religious gazetteer with target lexicons appropriate to racial, ethnic, or political hate speech, and adapting the frame taxonomy accordingly, would test the framework's generalisability and potentially establish it as a reusable template for structured hate speech interpretation across domains.
