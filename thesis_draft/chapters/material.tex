\section{Overview}
\label{sec:data-overview}

This study constructs a multilingual corpus of anti-Christian hate speech across three languages. The English component is assembled from four existing annotated resources --- HateXplain \citep{mathew_hatexplain_2021}, Jigsaw \citep{jigsaw-unintended-bias}, MLMA \citep{mlma}, and Measuring Hate Speech \citep{measuring-hate-speech} --- by filtering for Christian-target examples and deduplicating, yielding 58,940 texts (Table~\ref{tab:english-sources}). No new annotation was performed for English; the resulting corpus also serves as the seed pool for cross-lingual augmentation.

The Chinese and Japanese corpora are built through a two-stage pipeline. In the first stage, candidate texts are retrieved from online platforms, manually annotated with two binary labels (topic relevance and hatefulness), and then filtered by fine-tuned language-specific classifiers to obtain a native corpus. In the second stage, representative English texts are localized into Chinese and Japanese by an LLM and filtered by the same classifiers before being merged with the native data. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} illustrates the overall workflow.

\section{Chinese Dataset}
\label{sec:zh-data}

\subsection{Native Data Collection}

Candidate texts were retrieved from Tieba and Weibo using a keyword list that combined neutral Christianity-related terms with expressions associated with anti-Christian hostility in Chinese online discourse. After initial inspection, Weibo was excluded because it contributed too few usable texts; Tieba became the sole platform. The raw pool of 78,874 texts was reduced to 15,181 through deduplication and relevance filtering.

A sample of 4,000 texts was then annotated in Label Studio \citep{labelstudio} with two binary labels: \texttt{christianity\_related} (whether the text is substantively concerned with Christianity) and \texttt{hate\_speech} (whether it expresses target-directed hostility). The two-step design matters because Christian vocabulary occurs frequently in neutral, descriptive, or ironic contexts. Of the 4,000 annotated items, 613 were labeled Christianity-related and 152 as hate speech, confirming that explicit anti-Christian content is rare even within a curated candidate pool.

A Chinese hate speech classifier was fine-tuned on these labels using THU-COAI RoBERTa-COLD \citep{thucoai-roberta-cold} and then applied to the full cleaned pool; only texts predicted as both Christianity-related and hateful were retained. This produced a native Chinese corpus of \textbf{1,070 texts}.

\subsection{Data Augmentation}

To expand stylistic and rhetorical coverage beyond what could be obtained from Tieba alone, this study employed LLM-based localization. An earlier design that used direct few-shot generation was not retained because its outputs were excessively repetitive; the final design instead anchors generation in diverse English seed texts sampled from the merged English corpus. The LLM was prompted to rewrite these seeds into culturally adapted Chinese --- preserving the underlying hostile logic while adopting lexical and register conventions typical of Chinese social media --- and the outputs were filtered by the same fine-tuned Chinese classifier. This step yielded 895 synthetic Chinese texts, which were merged with the native set to form a final Chinese corpus of \textbf{1,965 texts}.

\section{Japanese Dataset}
\label{sec:ja-data}

\subsection{Native Data Collection}

The Japanese pipeline followed the same procedure. Candidate texts were retrieved from 5ch and X; X was subsequently excluded due to access-quota constraints that prevented building a working pool of sufficient size. The raw pool (43,101 texts) was cleaned to 4,181.

Manual annotation in Label Studio covered 4,084 Japanese texts with the same two-label scheme. Of these, 690 were labeled Christianity-related and 102 as hate speech (hate ratio: 2.5\%, somewhat lower than Chinese). A LUKE-based Japanese classifier \citep{luke-japanese} was fine-tuned on these labels and applied to the cleaned pool; 1,445 texts predicted as both Christianity-related and hateful formed the native Japanese corpus (\textbf{1,445 texts}).

\subsection{Data Augmentation}

LLM-based localization for Japanese followed the same seed-based procedure, with the generation prompt adjusted to target register conventions common in Japanese online forums and on X \citep{cross-lingual-llm-localization, llm-style-transfer}. Post-generation filtering with the fine-tuned Japanese classifier retained 915 synthetic texts, which were merged with the native set to yield a final Japanese corpus of \textbf{2,360 texts}.

\subsection{Lexical Profile}

To characterise the topical focus of each corpus, we extracted high-frequency content terms after language-specific tokenization (jieba for Chinese, janome for Japanese, TweetTokenizer for English), retaining only substantive words by filtering out function words, particles, and punctuation. Table~\ref{tab:top-keywords} reports the ten most frequent content terms per language.

\label{tab:top-keywords}
\end{table}

The Chinese corpus is anchored by core religious referents --- God, Jesus, Christianity, Bible --- alongside institutional labels such as \textit{church} and \textit{priest}. The presence of \textit{China} among the top terms suggests a framing that links Christianity to national identity or foreign-influence narratives.

The Japanese corpus is most distinctively characterised by \textit{Unification Church} (\textit{Toitsu Kyokai}), reflecting the heightened political salience of this organisation following the 2022 assassination of former Prime Minister Abe. This concentration indicates that Japanese anti-Christian discourse in the corpus is heavily intertwined with a specific domestic political event rather than with theological opposition alone. The co-occurrence of \textit{Abe} as the sixth most frequent term reinforces this reading. Broader Christian framing is nonetheless present in the remaining terms: Christ, church, religion, believer, Bible.

In English, the highest-frequency terms cluster around institutional Catholicism --- \textit{church}, \textit{catholic}, \textit{pope}, \textit{catholics}, \textit{bishops} --- reflecting the composition of the source corpora, in which abuse scandals and Catholic institutional authority are prominent themes in online anti-Christian discourse.
