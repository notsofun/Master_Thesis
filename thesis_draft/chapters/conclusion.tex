\chapter{Conclusion}%
\label{chap:conclusion}

This thesis set out to investigate whether cross-lingual hate speech analysis could move beyond the binary, English-centred paradigm that has dominated the field. The preceding chapters have assembled evidence bearing on that question across a trilingual corpus of anti-Christian online discourse. This chapter draws together the answers to the three research questions, states what the study contributes and to whom, and situates the work honestly within its own limits. Detailed theoretical and practical implications, specific future research directions, and component-level error analysis are addressed in Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion}; what follows is the final, high-level account.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Answers to the Research Questions}
\label{sec:conclusion-rq}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Current hate speech research suffers from a double narrowness: it concentrates on a handful of high-resource target categories in English, and it frames detection as a binary classification problem that collapses structured hostility into a single label. The three research questions posed in Chapter~\ref{chap:intro} were designed to probe whether a structured, multilingual approach could do better. The evidence assembled here indicates that it can, within the scope and constraints of the present study.

\textbf{RQ1 --- Who is targeted?} The three language communities share a transnational core of targets (Jesus, the Pope, generic Christian identity labels) but diverge sharply in the local figures and institutions they foreground. The Japanese corpus is dominated by the Unification Church and figures from the Abe--LDP scandal; the Chinese corpus concentrates on the clerical hierarchy and institutional Church; the English corpus distributes attention across theological figures, clergy, and political actors. Target-type composition --- individual clergy, political institution, or collective identity label --- differs systematically across language communities and turns out to be a structurally important variable for everything that follows.

\textbf{RQ2 --- How is hatred expressed?} Attack frame selection is systematically associated with target social category rather than varying freely across languages. Clerical role targets attract the Sexual Abuse Frame; political institutions attract Political Interference and Economic Exploitation; collective identity labels attract Rhetorical Attack and Moral Accusation. This target-to-frame alignment holds across all three language communities despite their otherwise divergent rhetorical traditions, and is consistent with the interpretation that hate speech partially operates according to genre-like, target-contingent scripts.

\textbf{RQ3 --- Why do people hate?} The moral motivation profiles diverge fundamentally between English and East Asian discourse. English scores negatively across all five classical Moral Foundation axes simultaneously --- a full-spectrum condemnation pattern. Chinese and Japanese score positively on those five axes but negatively on the Symbolic and Realistic Threat axes, a profile more consistent with identity-threat framing than with moral condemnation. The cross-topic robustness of this divergence reduces, though does not eliminate, the possibility of a pure dictionary artefact. Subject to that caveat, the pattern suggests that English and East Asian speakers are not merely expressing different intensities of the same hostility --- they are reaching for structurally different moral logics.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:conclusion-contributions}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{An empirical contribution to an under-studied domain.} Prior corpus-based work on religious hate speech has overwhelmingly focused on antisemitism and Islamophobia in English. Anti-Christian hostility, despite its global reach and presence across typologically distant online communities, has attracted almost no systematic computational attention. The trilingual corpus assembled here --- annotated with target, frame, and moral-axis layers --- provides a new empirical resource for studying digital-era transnational religious conflict.

\textbf{A methodological contribution to cross-lingual hate speech research.} The who--how--why pipeline provides evidence that moving beyond binary detection is feasible without requiring human-curated parallel annotations or translation-based mediation. The unsupervised thematic scaffold allows culture-specific discourse (the Unification Church scandal, Chinese civilisational incompatibility framing) to surface on its own terms rather than being forced into English-derived categories. The three-layer target extraction architecture treats zero-subject constructions in Chinese and Japanese as first-class design constraints. Together, these choices appear to yield a level of cross-lingual analytical resolution that binary detection approaches are not designed to provide.

\textbf{A reusable engineering toolkit.} The pipeline components --- the domain-specific trilingual gazetteer, the ten-class attack-frame taxonomy, and the LLM-assisted back-translation augmentation strategy --- are substitutable modules not tied to the Christianity domain. The complete codebase is released as open-source software at \url{https://github.com/notsofun/Master_Thesis}, enabling replication, domain transfer, or integration into existing content-moderation workflows.

\section{Limitations and Outlook}
\label{sec:conclusion-outlook}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Two structural limitations bound the conclusions above. First, the corpus is a cross-sectional snapshot: it documents the distribution of discourse at a given moment but cannot trace how triggering events reshape that distribution over time. Second, the keyword-based retrieval strategy captures explicit, surface-visible antagonism and is blind to hostility expressed through irony, code-switching, or community-specific vocabulary that does not intersect the seed keyword set. Both limitations are discussed in detail, along with four concrete future research directions, in Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion}.

These constraints make clear that the present study opens a research programme rather than closes one. The evidence assembled here indicates that structured, cross-lingual analysis of implicit religious hostility is tractable --- that the discourse has shape, that the shape is cross-linguistically legible, and that it differs from community to community in ways that matter for both detection systems and content policy. Whether those patterns generalise beyond Christianity, beyond the 2022--2023 snapshot, or beyond the three languages studied here are questions this work is not equipped to answer, but may now be positioned to ask.
