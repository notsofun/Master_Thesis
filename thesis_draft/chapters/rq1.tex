\chapter{RQ1: Who Are the Targets of Online Hate Speech?}%
\label{chap:who}

Building on the BERTopic discourse classification described in Chapter~\ref{chap:bertopic} and the target extraction pipeline detailed in Chapter~\ref{chap:methods}, the following results address the first research question: \textit{who} or \textit{what} is targeted in online hate speech about Christianity across English, Chinese, and Japanese communities. Using the 47 extracted topics as the unit of analysis, we identify the most frequently mentioned target entities per language community, examine how target types vary across topical contexts, and compare the structural composition of targets between languages.

%---------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Top Hate Targets by Language Community}
\label{sec:rq1-top-targets}

Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-top20} shows the twenty most frequently mentioned hate-speech targets in each language community, with original-language labels alongside English translations. Although the three communities share a common religious subject matter, the composition of their top targets diverges considerably.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{RQ1/rq1_global_top20_bilingual.png}
    \caption{Top 20 hate-speech targets per language community (EN, ZH, JA), ranked by total mention frequency across all topics. Labels are shown in the original language with English translations.}
    \label{fig:rq1-top20}
\end{figure}

\textbf{English.}
The English top-20 is anchored by Jesus (216 mentions), followed by denominational identity labels --- \textit{Catholic} (145), \textit{Christian} (125), \textit{Catholics} (118) --- and then \textit{Trump} (104) as a distinctly political target. Clerical role-categories (\textit{priests}, 81; \textit{bishops}, 52) and civic-political referents (\textit{Canada}, 30; \textit{LGBT}/\textit{LGBTQ}, 8/4) round out the list. The co-presence of Catholic media outlets \textit{NCR} (National Catholic Reporter, 24) and the abbreviation \textit{MSW} (Michael Sean Winters, 5) suggests that English-speaking discussants partly target journalistic voices within the Church rather than solely institutional actors. The overall picture is of a discourse that freely mixes theological critique (Jesus, the Church) with contemporary political figures and civil-rights framing.

\textbf{Chinese.}
In Chinese-language posts, 耶稣 (\textit{Jesus}, 217) is the most frequently targeted individual, but the list is dominated by role-based and institutional targets: 教会 (\textit{church}, 276 --- the highest count in the Chinese sub-corpus), 神父 (\textit{priest}, 170), 主教 (\textit{bishop}, 86), 信徒 (\textit{believers}, 97), and 修女 (\textit{nuns}, 52). A notable entry absent from the other two languages is 神棍 (\textit{religious fraudster/charlatan}, 52) --- a colloquial, pejorative compound that frames clergy not merely as morally deficient but as deliberate imposters. The high frequency of 教皇 (\textit{Pope}, 55) --- appearing alongside 教皇方济各 (\textit{Pope Francis}, 34) as a more specific variant --- extends the target profile upward through the clerical hierarchy, indicating that Chinese-language criticism does not stop at parish-level clergy but reaches the apex of Catholic institutional authority. Chinese targets thus skew toward the clerical hierarchy and institutional structures of the Church, framed in a register that foregrounds deception and exploitation of ordinary believers.

\textbf{Japanese.}
The Japanese list is structurally different from both its counterparts. 統一教会 (\textit{Unification Church / Family Federation for World Peace and Unification}, 501) dwarfs every other target in the entire dataset, reflecting the near-total dominance of Topic~0 in Japanese-language posts. It is followed immediately by 安倍晋三 (\textit{Shinzo Abe}, 98) and 自民党 (\textit{Liberal Democratic Party}, 76) --- neither of which has any equivalent in the English or Chinese top-20. 韓国 (\textit{South Korea}, 16) appears as a geopolitical entity, encoding the Church's foreign origins as part of the critique. Only from the seventh position onward do generic religious labels appear (キリスト教 [\textit{Christianity}], カトリック [\textit{Catholicism}], トランプ [\textit{Trump}], イエス・キリスト [\textit{Jesus Christ}]). This ordering makes clear that Japanese hate speech about religion is, in its most prevalent form, a discourse about domestic political corruption rather than theological controversy.

\textbf{Cross-lingual commonalities and divergences.}
All three communities target Jesus/耶稣/イエス・キリスト and some form of Catholic identity label, confirming that there exists a shared transnational core of religiously-grounded hostility. However, the framing diverges sharply: English targets combine doctrinal and political critique; Chinese targets foreground clerical hierarchy and institutional exploitation; Japanese targets are overwhelmingly anchored in a nationally-specific political scandal. The presence of Trump in both the English (\textit{Trump}, 104) and Japanese (トランプ, 42) top-20s but not in Chinese is a secondary cross-lingual link, suggesting that the global figure of Trump activates religious hostility in two of the three communities but not the third.

\section{Target Category Distribution by Topic}
\label{sec:rq1-by-topic}

To understand how the \textit{type} of target shifts across different discursive contexts, Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-stacked} presents, for each of the 47 topics, the distribution of the five LLM-assigned fine-grained target categories --- \textit{Religious Group}, \textit{Organization}, \textit{Specific Person}, \textit{Social Identity}, and \textit{Political Group} --- stacked by entity count and ordered by topic size (descending). Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-topic-targets} complements this overview by displaying, for the 24 largest topics, the actual top-$N$ entities per topic with bilingual labels, making the category-level patterns concrete.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{RQ1/rq1_topic_category_stacked.png}
    \caption{Target category distribution by topic. Each bar represents one of the 47 topics, stacked by entity-count contribution from five LLM-assigned category types: \textit{Religious Group}, \textit{Organization}, \textit{Specific Person}, \textit{Social Identity}, and \textit{Political Group}. Topics are ordered by size (descending).}
    \label{fig:rq1-stacked}
\end{figure}

\begin{sidewaysfigure}
    \centering
    % width 设置为 \textwidth 即可填满旋转后的页面高度
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{RQ1/rq1_topic_top_targets_bilingual.png}
    
    \caption{Top-$N$ hate-speech targets per topic for the 24 largest topics, with bilingual labels (original language + English translation). Bar colours encode entity category; topic identifiers and document counts are shown in each panel header.}
    \label{fig:rq1-topic-targets}
\end{sidewaysfigure}

Across all 47 topics and 4{,}688 LLM-categorised entity mentions, \textit{Social Identity} is the most frequent category overall (1{,}479 mentions, 31.5\%), followed by \textit{Organization} (1{,}322, 28.2\%), \textit{Specific Person} (935, 19.9\%), \textit{Religious Group} (676, 14.4\%), and \textit{Political Group} (276, 5.9\%). At the topic level, \textit{Social Identity} is the dominant category in 23 of the 47 topics; \textit{Religious Group} leads in 10; \textit{Organization} and \textit{Specific Person} each lead in 7; and \textit{Political Group} does not lead any topic independently. The prevalence of \textit{Social Identity} across the majority of topics reflects a discourse that primarily frames its objects of hostility as role-based or membership-based collectives rather than named institutions or individuals.

\textbf{Social Identity-dominant topics.}
The 23 topics where \textit{Social Identity} achieves the plurality vary considerably in the intensity of that dominance. Topic~26 ($n=38$) is the most concentrated, with 83\% of entity mentions falling into \textit{Social Identity}, reflecting a discourse organised entirely around membership-role categories (修道院 [\textit{monastery/convent}]) with virtually no named actors or institutions. Topic~20 ($n=47$, convent humour and anti-clerical satire) reaches 76\% \textit{Social Identity}, targeting role labels such as 修道院 and clerical collectives. Topic~3 ($n=149$, LGBTQ+ and Catholic teaching) sits at 57\% \textit{Social Identity}, with targets like 同性恋 (\textit{homosexuals}), LGBT, and 信徒 (\textit{believers}) framing a conflict over contested social identities. Topics~2 ($n=208$, gender and women in the Church) and~6 ($n=122$, clerical abuse) are Social Identity-dominant at 53\% and 46\% respectively, but each retains a notable \textit{Organization} share, reflecting discourses in which both the institutional hierarchy and the membership categories of its victims are simultaneously under scrutiny.

\textbf{Specific Person-dominant topics.}
Seven topics are anchored by named or historically personalised individuals. Topic~9 ($n=103$, Christological mockery) is the most concentrated: 64\% of entity mentions are \textit{Specific Person}, because 耶稣 / イエス・キリスト (\textit{Jesus}) absorbs the great majority of targeting. Topic~28 ($n=35$, the Burke--Francis intra-Church rift) reaches 57\% \textit{Specific Person}, naming Cardinal Burke (伯克 / バーク) and Pope Francis (教皇方济各 / フランシスコ教皇) as opposing protagonists. Topic~1 ($n=312$, Trump and Christian nationalism) is the largest \textit{Specific Person}-dominant topic at 34\%, with Trump as the focal target drawing together Catholic, evangelical, and politically-inflected hostility across all three language communities alongside substantial \textit{Social Identity} (28\%) and \textit{Religious Group} (18\%) co-mentions. Topic~44 ($n=21$, Pope Francis and cardinals) concentrates 42\% of its entity mentions on named clerical figures: 教皇, 方济各, 红衣主教 (\textit{cardinals}).

\textbf{Organization-dominant topics.}
Seven topics are led by \textit{Organization}. Topic~0 ($n=499$, Unification Church) is by far the largest: 48\% of its 1{,}238 entity mentions are \textit{Organization} --- 統一教会 (\textit{Unification Church}), 自民党 (\textit{LDP}), 創価学会 (\textit{S\={o}ka Gakkai}) --- while \textit{Specific Person} (24\%, driven by 安倍晋三 [\textit{Shinzo Abe}]) and \textit{Political Group} (13\%) fill secondary roles, producing a targeting structure that simultaneously indicts a religious institution, its political patron, and the party machine that enabled both. Topic~13 ($n=75$, religious education in public schools) and Topic~21 ($n=45$, denominational comparisons) reach 50\% and 47\% \textit{Organization} respectively, framing their discourse around institutional structures and their social functions. Topic~30 ($n=30$, Christian denominations) is the most concentrated Organization-dominant topic among the smaller topics at 53\%, targeting denominational structures themselves: 新教 (\textit{Protestantism}), 天主教 (\textit{Catholicism}), 东正教 (\textit{Eastern Orthodoxy}).

\textbf{Religious Group-dominant topics.}
Ten topics are led by \textit{Religious Group}. Topic~23 ($n=42$, Japanese-language critique of Christianity) is the most concentrated at 87\%, targeting キリスト教 (\textit{Christianity}) and キリスト教徒 (\textit{Christians}) as an undifferentiated religious collective. Topic~41 ($n=22$, criticism of Catholicism as a trap) reaches 67\% \textit{Religious Group} (カトリック, \textit{Catholics}), and Topic~15 ($n=65$, Catholic identity conflicts) reaches 60\%. Topics~11 ($n=81$, Islam and terrorism framing, 56\% \textit{Religious Group}) and~17 ($n=60$, end-times discourse, 49\%) concentrate on religious community labels --- イスラム (\textit{Islam}), テロ (\textit{terrorist}), 基督徒 (\textit{Christians}) --- without singling out individual actors or specific institutions. These topics share a register of \textit{collective indictment}: hostility is directed at a faith tradition or its adherents as a category.

\textbf{Cross-topic patterns.}
Several structural regularities emerge when the stacked profiles in Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-stacked} are read alongside the entity-level detail in Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-topic-targets}. First, \textit{Political Group} never achieves topic-level dominance on its own, but it functions as a consistent secondary presence in Topics~0, 1, 14, 16, 25, 31, and~36 --- all involving explicit intersections of religion with electoral or party politics --- reaching its highest absolute count (157 mentions) in Topic~0 due to the LDP's role in the Abe--Unification Church scandal. Second, topics in which \textit{Specific Person} is dominant tend to be more homogeneous in entity composition: a named individual anchors the discourse, causing other entity types to recede. Third, the smaller topics (below $n=30$) in Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-stacked} are almost uniformly thin in absolute entity count, making category-level proportions volatile; interpretive weight should rest on the larger topics where distributional patterns are more stable.

\section{Target Category Distribution by Language}
\label{sec:rq1-by-language}

Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-heatmap} aggregates the target category composition at the language level, expressing each category as a percentage of all entity mentions in posts of that language. The heatmap reveals three structurally distinct target profiles that map onto the three communities.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{RQ1/rq1_lang_category_heatmap.png}
    \caption{Target category distribution by language (\%). Rows represent language communities (EN, JA, ZH); columns represent entity categories. Cell values indicate the percentage of all entity mentions in a given language that fall into each category. Colour intensity reflects percentage magnitude.}
    \label{fig:rq1-heatmap}
\end{figure}


\textbf{English: individual persons and collective identity labels.}
English-language posts are dominated by two categories that together account for over half of all entity mentions: \texttt{PERSON} (30.4\%) and \texttt{NORP} (25.8\%). The high \texttt{PERSON} share reflects the breadth of spaCy's person-entity detection across English text, capturing both religious figures (Jesus, Pope Francis) and contemporary political actors (Trump, Trudeau, Harper) without further disambiguation. \texttt{NORP} encodes the parallel targeting of broad religiopolitical collectivities --- \textit{Catholics}, \textit{Christians}, \textit{Evangelicals} --- as named groups. \texttt{ORG} (14.8\%) and \textit{Social Identity} (12.6\%) add institutional and lay-identity layers (\textit{the Church, the Vatican}; \textit{believers, progressives, conservatives}). By contrast, \textit{Specific Person} (3.0\%) and \textit{Political Group} (0.2\%) are minimal, suggesting that English posts rely on spaCy-extracted person and group labels rather than the LLM-enriched distinctions: named individuals are captured as generic \texttt{PERSON} entities rather than flagged as uniquely prominent figures. Overall, English hate-speech targeting is \textit{broad and identity-anchored}: it mentions individuals and groups in roughly equal measure, without concentrating sharply on any single actor or institution.

\textbf{Japanese: organisational and individual precision.}
The Japanese profile is structurally the most distinctive, and also the most internally coherent: the spaCy categories \texttt{NORP}, \texttt{ORG}, and \texttt{PERSON} each register at exactly 0\%, because spaCy's English- and Chinese-trained models do not generalise to Japanese text. All categorisation therefore derives from the LLM-enrichment step, which produces a strikingly concentrated pattern. \textit{Organization} (33.8\%) is the single largest category in the entire matrix, driven overwhelmingly by 統一教会 (\textit{Unification Church}) and 自民党 (\textit{LDP}) in Topic~0. \textit{Specific Person} (23.9\%) is the second largest, capturing 安倍晋三 (\textit{Shinzo Abe}) as a named individual. Together, these two categories reflect a targeting logic that simultaneously indicts an institution and its most prominent human enabler --- a \textit{dual-precision} pattern unique to Japanese. \textit{Social Identity} (17.5\%), \textit{Religious Group} (14.1\%), and \textit{Political Group} (9.8\%) fill out the remaining shares, the last of these being the highest Political Group value across all three languages, reflecting the explicit party-political dimension of the Abe--Unification Church controversy.

\textbf{Chinese: social identity at the centre, institutional targeting alongside.}
Chinese posts show the highest \textit{Social Identity} proportion of any language (20.6\%), reflecting a targeting strategy centred on role-based membership categories: 基督徒 (\textit{Christians}), 信徒 (\textit{believers}), 神棍 (\textit{religious fraudsters}), 修女 (\textit{nuns}). Unlike Japanese, Chinese text is partially processed by spaCy, yielding substantial \texttt{PERSON} (18.5\%) and \texttt{ORG} (15.5\%) shares alongside the LLM-enriched \textit{Organization} (12.4\%) and \texttt{NORP} (12.7\%). The dual presence of spaCy and LLM categories produces a more evenly distributed profile than either English or Japanese. \textit{Religious Group} (7.2\%) and \textit{Specific Person} (6.8\%) are moderate but consistently present, the former driven by topics that frame Christianity comparatively against Buddhism, Taoism, and Islam (Topics~37 and~38), the latter by the occasional targeting of named clerical or political figures. Overall, Chinese hate speech constructs its targets primarily as \textit{types of people} defined by religious affiliation or institutional role, layered over an institutional critique captured by the ORG and Organization categories.

\textbf{Synthesis.}
When categories are collapsed into macro-types, group-level targeting (NORP, Social Identity, Religious Group, Political Group combined) accounts for a remarkably consistent share across all three languages: approximately 41\% for both English and Japanese, and 41\% for Chinese. The apparent cross-lingual similarity, however, conceals sharp differences in \textit{which} subcategories drive this share. In English, group targeting is dominated by \texttt{NORP} (collective identity labels detected by spaCy); in Japanese, it is driven by \textit{Social Identity} and \textit{Religious Group} (LLM-enriched role categories); in Chinese, it is distributed across both \texttt{NORP} and \textit{Social Identity}. The contrasts are sharpest for institutional and individual targeting: Japanese uniquely concentrates on a named organisation (\textit{Organization}, 34\%) paired with a named person (\textit{Specific Person}, 24\%), while English spreads individual mentions broadly via the generic \texttt{PERSON} category (30\%), and Chinese blends both approaches. This tripartite structure confirms that online hate speech about Christianity is not a uniform cross-cultural phenomenon, but is shaped by the specific political events, linguistic conventions, and NER capabilities that structure how each community names and frames its objects of hostility.
