\chapter{Introduction}%
\label{chap:intro}

\section{Motivation}

Computational research on online hate speech has grown rapidly, but its centre of gravity remains uneven. The literature is still dominated by English-language datasets, explicit lexical abuse, and binary detection tasks that classify a text as hateful or not hateful \citep{jahan_systematic_2023, ramos_comprehensive_2024}. This orientation has produced useful benchmarks and models, yet it leaves important parts of the problem weakly addressed. Religious hate speech is one such case. Within that domain, work has concentrated mainly on Islamophobia and antisemitism, while anti-Christian hostility remains comparatively underrepresented, especially outside English \citep{albadi_are_2018, vidgen_detecting_2020, Evolvi2018, mathew_hatexplain_2021, mollas_ethos_2022}.

This gap is not only empirical but also technical. Religious hostility is often expressed implicitly rather than through explicit slurs. It may be conveyed through accusation, irony, framing, or narratives of threat, corruption, and foreign influence \citep{bilewicz_hate_2020, paz_hate_2020}. Such discourse is difficult to capture with keyword-based approaches and only partially addressed by binary labels. The difficulty increases in cross-lingual settings, where Chinese and Japanese remain underrepresented in multilingual hate speech research and where semantically similar forms of hostility may be realised through different lexical and syntactic patterns \citep{corazza_multilingual_2020, joshi2020state}. For a computational linguistics thesis, the problem is therefore not simply whether hate speech can be detected, but how transformer-based multilingual methods can be used to analyse hateful discourse across languages while retaining interpretability.

A third dimension of the gap is less often noted: even where cross-lingual detection succeeds, it remains unclear whether speakers in different language communities articulate the same hatred for the same moral reasons. Whether anti-religious hostility in East Asian and Anglophone contexts rests on the same evaluative premises, or on structurally different moral architectures, is a question that binary detection cannot address.

This thesis addresses that problem by developing a transformer-based, cross-lingual analytical pipeline for religious hate speech detection and structured analysis. The aim is not to propose a new model architecture. Rather, the thesis combines multilingual semantic representations, cross-lingual alignment, and downstream interpretive analysis in order to move from detection toward a more structured account of hate speech. The proposed framework decomposes hateful discourse into three linked questions: who is targeted, how hostility is expressed, and why particular forms of hostility are sustained. The case study used to develop and assess this framework is anti-Christian hate speech in Chinese, Japanese, and English online discourse.

\section{Background}

\subsection{Religious Hate Speech Detection Beyond Binary Classification}

Online hate speech has attracted sustained attention because digital platforms amplify the scale, speed, and persistence of hostile discourse \citep{castano-pulgarin_internet_2021, matamoros-fernandez_racism_2021}. Religious hate speech is a particularly consequential subtype because it targets communities organised around shared belief and collective identity \citep{bilewicz_hate_2020, paz_hate_2020}. Computational work has responded with annotated corpora, supervised classifiers, and comparative benchmarks \citep{jahan_systematic_2023, ramos_comprehensive_2024}. These advances have established detection as the field's dominant task.

However, a binary detection paradigm is not sufficient for all research goals. Classifying a post as hateful does not in itself explain what kind of target is being attacked, what rhetorical strategy is used, or what justificatory logic organises the attack. These questions matter especially when hate is implicit. A post may not contain overt abuse, yet still construct a religious group as socially dangerous, politically subversive, morally corrupt, or civilisationally alien. From the perspective of computational linguistics, this creates a need for methods that retain the strengths of transformer-based semantic modelling while producing outputs that are more interpretable than a binary label alone \citep{elsherief_latent_2021, ollagnier_unsupervised_2023}.

This limitation is visible in the current distribution of research effort. Work on religious hate speech has focused primarily on targets that are well represented in English-language datasets, particularly Islamophobia and antisemitism \citep{albadi_are_2018, vidgen_detecting_2020, Evolvi2018}. Anti-Christian discourse has received much less systematic attention, even though sociological work suggests that anti-Christian bias and hostility are observable in multiple settings \citep{Yancey2014, scheitle2020} and global monitoring reports show that Christians experience harassment in a large number of countries \citep{pew_rising_restrictions_2019}. As a result, both the data resources and the analytical frameworks for anti-Christian hate speech remain comparatively underdeveloped.

\subsection{Cross-Lingual and Implicit Hate as a Computational Problem}

The challenge becomes sharper in multilingual settings. Cross-lingual hate speech research has shown that multilingual encoders can support transfer across languages, but performance remains uneven and is still heavily shaped by data availability and language representation in benchmarks \citep{corazza_multilingual_2020, joshi2020state}. Chinese and Japanese are particularly important in this respect because they are both typologically distinct from English and relatively underrepresented in existing work. This means that a study spanning Chinese, Japanese, and English must contend not only with domain differences, but also with the technical problem of aligning semantically comparable discourse across languages. A further structural challenge, underrepresented in existing multilingual benchmarks, is the pervasiveness of zero-subject constructions in Chinese and Japanese: in contexts where the referent is recoverable from prior discourse, both languages routinely omit the grammatical subject, rendering standard dependency-based entity extraction unreliable.

Implicit hate further complicates the task. In many cases, hostility is carried not by explicit insults but by recurrent framings: a religious group may be described as manipulative, foreign-controlled, hypocritical, abusive, or socially parasitic. These signals are often distributed across phrases, propositions, and discourse structure rather than encoded in isolated keywords. A purely lexical approach is therefore inadequate, while a purely predictive approach risks sacrificing interpretability. What is needed is a cross-lingual NLP design that can preserve semantic comparability while also supporting structured analysis of targets, rhetorical forms, and underlying motivations.

\subsection{Anti-Christian Hate Speech as an Empirical Testbed}

Anti-Christian discourse provides a useful empirical setting in which to address these questions. Christianity is globally widespread, but its social positioning differs sharply across linguistic and political contexts. In many English-speaking settings, anti-Christian rhetoric is articulated against a religion that remains culturally influential \citep{Yancey2014, scheitle2020}. In China and Japan, by contrast, Christianity is more likely to be discussed through the language of foreign influence, cultural disloyalty, social manipulation, or institutional harm, shaped by distinct histories of missionisation, nationalism, and state regulation \citep{yang2011religion, dunch_beyond_2002, mullins1998christianity, mullins2021religion}. Japan's context is further complicated by recent political developments, particularly the relationship between the Unification Church and the Liberal Democratic Party that came under intense public scrutiny following the 2022 assassination of Shinzo Abe --- a context in which religious hostility and political critique become structurally entangled.

These differences make anti-Christian hate speech more than a substantive case study. They make it a demanding testbed for cross-lingual computational analysis. The same broad target category appears across languages, but the discourse surrounding it is historically specific and often implicitly hostile. Studying Chinese, Japanese, and English materials together therefore allows this thesis to address a computational linguistics problem with broader relevance: how to analyse religious hate speech across languages in a way that is both semantically robust and analytically interpretable.

\section{Research Gap}

Taken together, the literature points to three related gaps. First, anti-Christian hate speech remains weakly represented in existing NLP resources, especially in Chinese and Japanese. Second, cross-lingual work on hate speech still offers limited coverage of non-European and lower-resource settings. Third, the dominant task formulation remains binary detection, which is not well suited to the analysis of implicit hate or to the production of structured, interpretable findings.

This thesis addresses these gaps by framing the problem as one of cross-lingual detection \emph{and} structured interpretation. Using transformer-based multilingual encoders as the semantic foundation, the thesis builds a reusable pipeline that first identifies relevant hateful discourse and then analyses it along three dimensions: the targets of hostility, the ways in which hostility is expressed, and the moral or ideological motivations associated with those expressions. In this sense, the thesis remains rooted in computational linguistics, but it uses computational methods to produce outputs that are informative for broader social analysis. The goal is not to replace detection, but to extend it.

\section{Research Questions}

The study is organised around three linked research questions. Together, they define a progression from detection and target identification to rhetorical analysis and, finally, to higher-level interpretation.

The first question concerns the objects of hostility. Anti-Christian discourse does not attack a single undifferentiated target. It may focus on institutions, clergy, ordinary believers, converts, or Christianity as a political and cultural formation. We therefore ask:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{RQ1: Who are the targets?} What entities---institutions, figures, or groups---are most frequently attacked in anti-Christian discourse across Chinese, Japanese, and English online texts, and how do these targets differ across languages?
\end{enumerate}

The second question concerns the form of expression. Hostility may be realised through slurs, but also through recurrent rhetorical and thematic frames such as accusations of manipulation, abuse, political interference, hypocrisy, or foreign allegiance. We therefore ask:

\begin{enumerate}
    \setcounter{enumi}{1}
    \item \textbf{RQ2: How is hatred expressed?} What are the dominant rhetorical and thematic attack frameworks used to articulate anti-Christian hostility, and do these patterns vary cross-linguistically?
\end{enumerate}

The third question concerns interpretation. Once targets and forms of expression have been identified, a further issue remains: what broader moral or ideological logics make these attacks persuasive or legitimate within each language community? Drawing on Moral Foundations Theory as an interpretive framework \citep{haidt2012righteous, graham_liberals_2009}, extended here with intergroup threat dimensions that capture identity-based rather than ethics-based hostility, we ask:

\begin{enumerate}
    \setcounter{enumi}{2}
    \item \textbf{RQ3: Why do people hate?} What underlying moral motivations, as characterised by Moral Foundations Theory, drive anti-Christian rhetoric in each language community, and what cross-linguistic differences can be observed in these motivations?
\end{enumerate}

Taken together, these questions operationalise a \emph{who--how--why} framework for cross-lingual hate speech analysis. In this thesis, that framework is applied to anti-Christian discourse, but it is intended as a reusable analytical design rather than a domain-specific description alone.

\section{Contributions}

The contributions of this thesis are threefold.

First, it makes a \textbf{dataset and resource contribution} by constructing the first trilingual corpus of anti-Christian hate speech in Chinese, Japanese, and English, accompanied by structured annotation across target, frame, and moral-axis dimensions. The Chinese and Japanese subsets are further expanded through an LLM-assisted back-translation augmentation strategy that substantially raises training data volume for two low-resource languages, and the full corpus together with the codebase is released as open-source at \url{https://github.com/notsofun/Master_Thesis}.

Second, it makes a \textbf{comparative empirical contribution} by providing the first systematic cross-lingual analysis of who is targeted, how hostility is rhetorically constructed, and what moral motivations drive it across three typologically and culturally distinct language communities. The analysis reveals two structural regularities that extend the existing literature: attack frame selection is systematically determined by the social category of the target rather than by language community, operating as a set of genre-like rhetorical conventions; and English and East Asian discourse are grounded in fundamentally different moral architectures, with Anglophone hostility constituting broad ethical condemnation and East Asian hostility constituting identity-threat framing.

Third, it makes a \textbf{methodological contribution} by assembling a reusable \emph{who--how--why} analytical pipeline for cross-lingual hate speech research. The contribution lies not in introducing a new neural architecture, but in showing how multilingual semantic representations, unsupervised topic modelling, structured predicate extraction, and moral-axis projection can be combined into a coherent framework that moves from binary detection toward interpretable, cross-linguistically comparable analysis. A key design commitment is to induce thematic structure from the corpus rather than imposing a predefined taxonomy, avoiding the risk of encoding English-language research assumptions at the cost of suppressing locally prominent phenomena.

\section{Thesis Structure}

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows. Chapter~\ref{chap:related-work} reviews the literature on religious hate speech, multilingual hate speech detection, cross-lingual NLP, and interpretive approaches beyond binary classification. Chapter~\ref{chap:material} describes the data sources, corpus construction, and annotation procedures used to identify religious hate speech across the three languages. Chapter~\ref{chap:methods} presents the transformer-based analytical pipeline, including multilingual semantic representation, cross-lingual alignment, and the procedures used to operationalise the \emph{who--how--why} framework. Chapter~\ref{chap:bertopic} introduces the topic structures that support the later analyses. Chapters~\ref{chap:who}--\ref{chap:why} then address the three research questions in turn. Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion} and Chapter~\ref{chap:conclusion} summarise the main findings, discusses limitations, and outlines directions for future research.
