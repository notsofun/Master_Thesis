\chapter{Discussion}%
\label{chap:discussion}

The preceding chapters addressed the three research questions of this thesis in sequence: who is targeted in anti-Christian online hate speech (Chapter~\ref{chap:who}), how that hostility is rhetorically constructed (Chapter~\ref{chap:how}), and what moral motivations underlie it across language communities (Chapter~\ref{chap:why}). This chapter synthesises the substantive findings into a coherent account of cross-lingual hate speech structure, evaluates what the analytical pipeline achieves and where it falls short as a computational tool, discusses the practical lessons from building low-resource CJK corpora, and considers how cultural specificity constrains standard NLP approaches. The chapter closes with a consolidated reflection on limitations and directions for future research.

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Synthesis of Key Findings}
\label{sec:discussion-synthesis}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The central question driving this thesis was whether anti-Christian hate speech can be decomposed into a structured, cross-linguistically comparable account of targets, rhetorical forms, and moral motivations. The empirical findings across RQ1--RQ3 suggest that it can --- but the structure that emerges is not uniform. Three distinct modes of hostility appear, each organised around a different social logic.

\textbf{Answering RQ1.} All three communities share a transnational targeting core: Jesus, Catholic identity labels, and the institutional Church appear as objects of hostility across Chinese, English, and Japanese alike. However, this shared surface conceals sharply divergent target compositions. English targeting tends to be broad, spanning theological figures, clergy, collective identity labels, and contemporary political actors. Chinese targeting concentrates on the clerical hierarchy and the Church as an institution, framed in a register of deception and exploitation. Japanese targeting is almost entirely absorbed by a single politically-situated narrative centred on the Unification Church and its domestic political associates.

\textbf{Answering RQ2.} Frame selection appears to be systematically shaped by the social category of the target rather than by language alone. Across all three communities, clerical targets attract the Sexual Abuse Frame; collective identity labels attract Rhetorical Attack and Moral Accusation; and the Japanese institutional--political target pair attracts Political Interference and Economic Exploitation. This target-to-frame alignment suggests that hate speech operates according to genre-like conventions in which the target's perceived social role constrains the available rhetorical repertoire.

\textbf{Answering RQ3.} The moral motivation analysis reveals the sharpest cross-lingual divergence. English-language discourse is negatively aligned across all five classical Moral Foundations axes simultaneously, constituting a broad moral condemnation. Chinese and Japanese discourse remain relatively normative on the classical foundations but shift sharply negative on the Symbolic Threat and Realistic Threat axes. This suggests that the English community indicts Christianity for moral failure within a shared ethical framework, while East Asian communities frame it as a danger to cultural identity or social cohesion --- a structurally different rhetorical mode that carries distinct implications for detection and moderation. One unexpected feature of the moral axis data --- the high empirical correlation among the five classical foundations ($r \approx 0.8$) --- is discussed further in Section~\ref{sec:discussion-pipeline}.

\section{The Who--How--Why Pipeline as a Computational Tool}
\label{sec:discussion-pipeline-contributions}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

A core claim of this thesis is methodological: the who--how--why pipeline offers a way to move computational hate speech research beyond binary detection toward structured sociological interpretation. This section evaluates that claim in relation to existing NLP work, with attention to both what the pipeline achieves and where it remains limited.

\subsection{What the Pipeline Adds Beyond Existing Approaches}

The dominant paradigm in computational hate speech research treats the task as binary classification \citep{jahan_systematic_2023, ramos_comprehensive_2024}. The current pipeline demonstrates that going beyond this binary is both feasible and analytically productive: the target extraction, frame classification, and moral motivation analysis stages each recover structured information that a detection-only label cannot provide. The finding that attack frame selection is determined by target category (Section~\ref{sec:rq2-by-target}) --- clergy attract abuse frames, institutions attract political and economic frames, collective identities attract rhetorical attack --- is precisely the kind of discourse-level regularity that is invisible to a classifier trained to return a single label.

This contribution builds directly on \citet{ollagnier_unsupervised_2023}'s unsupervised pipeline, which introduced topic clustering as a tool for hate speech analysis but stopped short of providing an interpretive account of what happens inside those clusters. The present thesis demonstrates that BERTopic topic membership can serve as an organisational scaffold for subsequent layers of analysis, rather than as an endpoint in itself.

The FrameAxis moral motivation layer \citep{Kwak_2021} offers an additional methodological contribution: it produces morally interpretable document-level scores at the scale of the full corpus without requiring a labelled moral-motivation training set, which would be prohibitively expensive to construct in three languages. The statistically significant cross-lingual differences on the Threat axes ($F$-statistics above 500, $\eta^2 > 0.24$) suggest that the projection approach carries genuine signal rather than noise, even given the dictionary approximations discussed below.

A practical strength of the overall design is its modularity. The gazetteer, frame taxonomy, and prompt templates are not hard-coded to Christianity. Substituting domain-appropriate lexicons and category definitions could adapt the pipeline to other hate speech targets --- racial, ethnic, or political --- without redesigning the architecture. This modularity was an explicit design goal and supports the claim to a methodological contribution beyond the specific empirical case.

\subsection{Cross-Lingual NLP: What Worked and What Required Careful Engineering}

Applying NLP tools across Chinese, Japanese, and English simultaneously raised a set of engineering challenges that standard monolingual pipelines do not face. Three deserve particular attention.

\textbf{Multilingual topic alignment.} Raw multilingual sentence embeddings, even from a shared model such as E5-large-instruct, tend to cluster by language rather than by topic, because surface-level lexical features create language-specific offsets in the embedding space. The centroid alignment procedure described in Chapter~\ref{chap:methods} --- subtracting the language-level centroid from each document vector before clustering --- was specifically designed to remove this offset. The resulting cross-lingual fusion rate in BERTopic provides empirical validation that Chinese, Japanese, and English documents on shared themes are being grouped together. This alignment step is not standard in the multilingual hate speech literature \citep{corazza_multilingual_2020} and may be of independent methodological interest for future cross-lingual topic modelling work.

\textbf{Zero-subject constructions.} Chinese and Japanese routinely omit grammatical subjects and objects in constructions where the referent is recoverable from context. This makes standard dependency-based Named Entity Recognition insufficient for identifying attack targets in these languages: many sentences that are clearly hostile toward a religious entity contain no parseable subject--verb--object triple. The three-layer RQ1 extraction (spaCy NER~$\to$~domain gazetteer~$\to$~LLM fallback) was designed specifically to handle this. The Gemini API fallback recovered targets in the structurally incomplete sentences where Stages~1 and~2 produced null results, addressing a structural challenge that is specific to CJK text and often overlooked in multilingual hate speech detection research \citep{joshi2020state}.

\textbf{Unexpected axis correlation.} The five classical Moral Foundations axes are theoretically and empirically distinguishable in survey-based MFT research \citep{graham2009liberals}, where liberals and conservatives show differentiated foundation profiles. In this corpus, however, the five foundations correlate highly ($r \approx 0.8$), effectively collapsing into a single ``moral condemnation'' dimension. This may reflect a genuine feature of hate speech as a communicative genre --- one that favours maximal moral delegitimation rather than selective moral emphasis --- or it may indicate that the FrameAxis dictionary-based projection lacks the lexical resolution to distinguish foundations in online registers. Disentangling these possibilities was beyond the scope of the present study but represents an important open question for future FrameAxis applications.

\subsection{Unsupervised Classification as a Design Choice}
\label{subsec:discussion-unsupervised}

A defining feature of this thesis is its reliance on \textit{unsupervised classification} --- BERTopic topic modelling --- as the thematic scaffold for the entire analytical pipeline. This design choice requires explicit evaluation, as it represents a deliberate departure from the supervised paradigms that dominate the hate speech detection literature \citep{jahan_systematic_2023}.

The core rationale is epistemological: any predefined taxonomy of hate speech topics imposed prior to analysis would embed assumptions about which themes matter in anti-Christian discourse across Chinese, Japanese, and English. These assumptions would almost inevitably be derived from English-language research traditions \citep{ollagnier_unsupervised_2023}, where topics such as clerical sexual abuse and culture-war politics are prominent. A fixed scheme would risk missing phenomena that are salient in the East Asian corpora but absent from the English-language literature --- such as the religion-as-political-proxy dynamic that dominates Japanese discourse or the civilisational incompatibility framing characteristic of Chinese online nationalism. BERTopic's inductive approach lets the corpus reveal its own thematic structure without this prior imposition.

The empirical results from Chapter~\ref{chap:bertopic} suggest that this approach achieved its intended goal. The 47 extracted topics are semantically well-separated --- confirmed by near-zero pairwise cosine similarities across topic embeddings --- indicating that the model has identified genuinely distinct discourse types rather than arbitrary partitions. More critically for a cross-lingual study, the model successfully identified both monolingual and cross-lingual topics without language supervision. Three topics --- the Conservative--Liberal Church rift (entropy~=~1.097), LGBTQ+ and Catholic Teaching (entropy~=~1.095), and the Science--Faith debate (entropy~=~1.092) --- show near-perfectly balanced language distributions, providing direct validation that the centroid alignment procedure effectively suppressed language-specific embedding offsets. Conversely, the monolingual topics (Topic~0 at 98.8\% Japanese; Topic~43 at 95.5\% Chinese; Topic~16 at 91.8\% English) reflect genuinely culture-specific discourses rather than alignment failure: they are monolingual because the phenomena they describe have no cross-community equivalent.

The primary cost of the unsupervised approach is interpretive labour and reproducibility uncertainty. The 47-topic solution depends on hyperparameter choices that carry real trade-offs between topic granularity and cluster stability. The 2,530 documents assigned to the noise class (Topic~$-1$) are excluded from all downstream analyses, introducing a systematic blind spot. And unlike a supervised classifier, BERTopic does not produce a fixed output: alternative configurations would yield a different set of topics. These are inherent constraints of density-based clustering, and they should be weighed explicitly against the inductive advantages when evaluating the overall methodological contribution.

\section{Corpus Construction: Lessons from Low-Resource CJK Settings}
\label{sec:discussion-corpus}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The corpus construction process documented in Chapter~\ref{chap:material} produced several findings that are relevant beyond this specific study, for researchers working on hate speech detection in low-resource or domain-specific settings.

\subsection{LLM Localisation as a Supplement, Not a Replacement, for Native Data}

An earlier design attempted to generate anti-Christian hate speech in Chinese and Japanese directly from a few-shot prompt with no seed text. This approach was discarded because the outputs were repetitive and lacked the topical and rhetorical diversity required for meaningful downstream analysis. The final augmentation strategy --- anchoring generation in semantically diverse English seed texts that were clustered and sampled to ensure topical coverage --- produced substantially more varied outputs, as evidenced by the 93.4\% passage rates through the Christianity-relevance and hate-speech classifiers for both languages.

This design lesson is consistent with emerging findings in the NLP literature on LLM data augmentation \citep{ramos_comprehensive_2024}: unconstrained generation by a large language model tends toward high-frequency surface patterns in the training distribution, producing texts that are fluent but semantically redundant. Grounding generation in diverse seed texts provides an external forcing mechanism that counteracts this tendency. Researchers planning LLM-based augmentation for domain-specific corpora should treat the seed selection and clustering step as a first-class design decision rather than a preprocessing detail.

\subsection{Class Imbalance and the Recall--Precision Trade-off}

Hate speech detection in low-resource CJK settings faces severe class imbalance. Even after careful keyword-based pre-filtering, manually annotated hate speech accounted for only 3.8\% of the Chinese annotation set and 2.5\% of the Japanese set. This imbalance has a direct practical consequence: a classifier trained on unbalanced data will achieve high accuracy by predicting the majority class, making minority-class recall --- the metric that matters for constructing a hate speech corpus --- very low.

Two interventions addressed this. First, Focal Loss \citep{lin2017focal} up-weighted hard-to-classify minority samples during training, reducing the dominance of the majority class in the gradient. Second, back-translation augmentation expanded the hate-speech training set by generating paraphrases through multiple pivot languages, raising the effective hate ratio from approximately 3\% to over 20\% in both languages. The resulting classifiers showed substantial improvements over their untuned baselines: from Hate F1~=~0.172 to 0.635 for Chinese, and from Hate F1~=~0.294 to 0.579 for Japanese at the best-performing epoch.

Critically, the corpus construction goal was not to maximise classification F1 in the conventional sense, but to maximise recall at an acceptable precision, so that the downstream analytical corpus captured the widest possible range of anti-Christian hate speech for interpretation. This diverges from the standard NLP benchmark optimisation target and reflects the epistemic requirements of computational social science: a corpus optimised for recall-priority analysis supports more complete qualitative coverage than one optimised for classification accuracy \citep{matamoros-fernandez_racism_2021}.

\subsection{The Validity Limits of Back-Translated Training Data}

Back-translation augmentation, while effective for addressing class imbalance, introduces a validity constraint that the present study cannot fully resolve. Texts generated by translating through multiple pivot languages may not accurately reproduce the idiomatic register, coded vocabulary, and culturally embedded irony that characterise Chinese and Japanese anti-Christian discourse in its native online form. In particular, the colloquial pejorative register of 5ch (Japanese) and Tieba (Chinese) --- characterised by abbreviations, wordplay, and community-specific coded terms --- is unlikely to be fully preserved through a translation chain that passes through English, Korean, French, and German.

The practical consequence is that the synthetic portion of the corpus (approximately 46\% of the Chinese corpus, 39\% of the Japanese corpus) may underrepresent some of the most culturally specific and computationally challenging forms of anti-Christian hate speech. Future corpus construction efforts in this domain would benefit from native-language augmentation strategies --- such as lexically constrained generation in the target language or retrieval-augmented generation from existing native corpora --- that do not rely on translation as an intermediate step.

\section{Cross-Cultural Specificity as a Computational Challenge}
\label{sec:discussion-culture}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The three-language comparative design of this thesis exposes a set of cross-cultural challenges that are not simply about language, but about the relationship between social context and what NLP tools can detect and classify reliably.

The most striking example is the Japanese corpus. The near-total dominance of Topic~0 (Unification Church / Abe scandal) in the Japanese data means that the pipeline's outputs for Japanese are structurally determined by a single major political event. The Political Interference and Economic Exploitation frames, the Threat-based moral motivation profile, and the specific target constellation (统一教会, 安倍晋三, 自民党) are all downstream consequences of this event's outsized presence in the corpus. This is not a failure of the pipeline; it is an accurate reflection of the discourse. But it does mean that conclusions about Japanese anti-Christian hate speech generalise primarily to the specific context of religion-as-political-scandal rather than to theological or cultural hostility more broadly.

A related challenge applies to the Chinese corpus. The category of 神棍 (\textit{religious fraudster}) has no straightforward equivalent in English or Japanese, and its classification behaviour under a taxonomy developed partly with reference to English-language hate speech literature raises questions about category transferability. More broadly, the domain gazetteer used in RQ1 was constructed partly from prior work on hate speech targets \citep{yoder_how_2022, thomson_identity-based_2021} developed in Western contexts. Some gazetteer categories may impose conceptual distinctions that are not natural to Chinese or Japanese religious discourse, while others may miss locally salient categories.

These challenges are not merely empirical inconveniences. They point toward a deeper methodological issue: NLP tools trained primarily on English data and evaluated on English benchmarks embed implicit assumptions about what constitutes a grammatical subject, a named entity, a slur, or a morally charged term. Applying such tools to Chinese and Japanese text requires active engineering effort (zero-subject handling, language-specific lexicons, adapted prompt design) and produces results that need to be interpreted with awareness of what may have been missed. This is not a limitation specific to the present study; it reflects the current state of multilingual hate speech research, where Chinese and Japanese remain significantly underrepresented in mainstream benchmarks \citep{joshi2020state, corazza_multilingual_2020}.

\section{Limitations and Directions for Future Research}
\label{sec:discussion-limitations}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

Several limitations of the present study should be acknowledged explicitly, along with their practical consequences for the conclusions that can be drawn.

\textbf{Corpus representativeness.} The keyword-based collection strategy captures only texts that employ surface-level markers of anti-Christian content. Implicit hate speech that avoids keyword triggers is systematically excluded, meaning that the frame and motivation profiles represent the keyword-legible portion of anti-Christian discourse rather than its full population. This likely leads to underestimation of the most culturally coded and rhetorically sophisticated forms of hostility.

\textbf{LLM non-determinism.} The Gemini API calls used for target fallback (RQ1) and frame classification (RQ2) introduce non-determinism: rerunning the pipeline may produce different target lists and frame assignments. The frame classification prompt was developed through pilot testing rather than validated against a gold-standard annotation set. A formal inter-rater reliability study between the LLM and human annotators on a held-out sample would strengthen confidence in the reported frame distributions and represents the most immediately addressable limitation of the present study.

\textbf{FrameAxis dictionary coverage.} The Chinese Moral Foundations Dictionary \citep{cheng_c-mfd_2023} does not provide explicit polarity annotations; polarity was inferred via negation-prefix rules, which may misclassify compound terms. The J-MFD \citep{matsuo2019development} was developed for general Japanese text and may underrepresent the vocabulary of online political discourse. Both dictionaries are likely to miss neologisms and coded terms that are characteristic of social media registers. These gaps suggest that the specific axis scores should be interpreted as approximate indicators of moral orientation rather than precise measurements.

\textbf{Cross-sectional design.} The corpus lacks high-resolution timestamps, meaning the analysis cannot track how specific triggering events shift discourse patterns over time. The moral motivation profiles reflect structural distributions across the corpus, not the dynamic processes by which those distributions are activated. A longitudinal extension using timestamped data and interrupted time-series analysis would allow researchers to measure how specific events --- such as the Abe assassination or individual clerical scandal revelations --- reshape the moral vocabulary of online hate discourse.

Looking forward, several directions emerge directly from these limitations. \textbf{Native-language augmentation strategies} --- lexically constrained generation in the target language, or retrieval-augmented generation from existing native-language corpora --- would reduce dependence on back-translated training data. \textbf{Platform-specific moral lexicons} for Chinese and Japanese online discourse, constructed from corpus data with human-validated polarity annotations, would improve the granularity and reliability of FrameAxis projections. \textbf{Cross-domain transfer of the framework} --- replacing the religious gazetteer and frame taxonomy with domain-appropriate equivalents for racial, ethnic, or political hate speech --- would test the pipeline's transferability and its potential as a general-purpose tool for structured hate speech interpretation. Finally, a formal \textbf{LLM annotation validation study}, establishing inter-rater agreement between Gemini-assigned frame labels and human judgements on a representative sample, would provide the calibrated reliability estimate that the current study lacks.
