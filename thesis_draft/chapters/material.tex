\chapter{Datasets}%
\label{chap:material}

This chapter describes how we built the multilingual corpus of anti-Christian hate speech used in this study. The corpus covers three languages: English, Chinese, and Japanese.

For English, we assembled texts from four existing annotated datasets --- HateXplain \citep{mathew_hatexplain_2021}, Jigsaw \citep{jigsaw-unintended-bias}, MLMA \citep{mlma}, and Measuring Hate Speech \citep{measuring-hate-speech} --- by filtering for Christianity-targeted content and removing duplicates. This produced 58,940 English texts (Table~\ref{tab:english-sources}). No new annotation was carried out for English; this corpus also serves as the seed pool for cross-lingual augmentation.

The Chinese and Japanese corpora were built from scratch through a two-stage pipeline. Stage~1 collects and annotates raw texts from online platforms, then trains language-specific classifiers to filter a native corpus. Stage~2 expands the corpus by translating representative English seed texts into the target language via an LLM, with the same classifiers used for quality filtering. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} gives a high-level overview of the full workflow, and the sections below describe each stage in detail.

\section{Chinese Dataset}
\label{sec:zh-data}
%===================================================================

\subsection{Stage 1: Native Data Collection}
\label{subsec:zh-native}

Raw candidate texts were collected from two Chinese social media platforms using a keyword list that combined neutral Christianity-related terms with expressions associated with anti-Christian hostility in Chinese online discourse.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Tieba} (Baidu's forum platform): 46,640 posts were crawled by feeding keywords into the search API.
  \item \textbf{Weibo and HuggingFace datasets}: 19,820 additional texts were gathered from four publicly available Weibo-derived datasets (\texttt{liyucheng/zhihu\_26k}, \texttt{vilarin/weibo-2014}, \texttt{m4rque2/weibo\_automobile}, \texttt{Giacinta/weibo}).
\end{itemize}

After combining and deduplicating, the raw pool contained 78,874 texts. Weibo-derived sources were subsequently excluded because they contributed too few usable texts after keyword filtering (only 412 relevant posts), leaving Tieba as the sole native platform. Relevance filtering reduced the pool to \textbf{15,181 texts}.

To select the most informative 4,000 texts for annotation, we applied an ensemble detection approach using multiple pre-trained models. The models were run on all 15,181 candidates and the 4,000 texts on which the ensemble was \emph{least confident} (i.e.\ highest prediction uncertainty or most disagreement between models) were selected for human review. This active-learning-style selection strategy ensures the annotation budget is spent on genuinely ambiguous cases, which improves classifier training later.

These 4,000 texts were then annotated in Label Studio \citep{labelstudio} with two binary labels:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{christianity\_related}: whether the post substantively concerns Christianity (not just a passing mention).
  \item \texttt{hate\_speech}: whether it expresses target-directed hostility toward Christians.
\end{itemize}

The two-label design matters because Christian vocabulary appears frequently in neutral or ironic contexts. Table~\ref{tab:annotation-distribution} shows the resulting label counts: only 613 of 4,000 posts were Christianity-related, and only 152 were hate speech --- confirming that explicit anti-Christian content is rare even within a carefully curated pool.

A Chinese hate speech classifier was then fine-tuned on these labels using THU-COAI RoBERTa-COLD \citep{thucoai-roberta-cold} as the backbone, with Focal Loss to address class imbalance. The fine-tuned classifier was applied to all 15,181 cleaned texts. Texts predicted as \emph{both} Christianity-related and hateful were retained, yielding a \textbf{native Chinese corpus of 1,070 texts} (1,687 predicted hateful; 3,296 predicted Christianity-related out of the full pool). Figure~\ref{fig:zh-funnel} illustrates the data reduction through each stage.

\subsection{Stage 2: LLM-Based Augmentation}
\label{subsec:zh-aug}

To extend stylistic and rhetorical coverage beyond what Tieba alone provides, we used LLM-based localization. An earlier design that generated hate speech directly from a few-shot prompt was discarded because outputs were repetitive and lacked diversity. The final approach instead anchors generation in diverse English seed texts.

The augmentation process worked as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Seed selection}: The 58,940 English texts were clustered in a shared embedding space. A representative sample of texts was selected from each cluster to ensure topical diversity.
  \item \textbf{LLM localization}: The LLM was prompted to rewrite each seed text into culturally adapted Chinese, preserving the hostile logic while adopting the lexical and register conventions of Chinese social media.
  \item \textbf{Classifier filtering}: The same fine-tuned Chinese classifier filtered the generated texts. 1,250 texts were processed; 1,167 (93.4\%) passed the hate speech filter and 941 (75.3\%) passed the Christianity-relevance filter. After requiring both conditions, \textbf{895 synthetic texts} were retained.
\end{enumerate}

The native and synthetic sets were merged to form the \textbf{final Chinese corpus of 1,965 texts}.

\section{Japanese Dataset}
\label{sec:ja-data}
%===================================================================

\subsection{Stage 1: Native Data Collection}
\label{subsec:ja-native}

The Japanese pipeline followed the same overall procedure. Raw texts were collected from two platforms:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{5ch} (Japan's largest anonymous bulletin board): 26,737 posts were crawled by keyword.
  \item \textbf{Common Crawl}: A targeted crawl of Japanese web text using religion-related keywords yielded 38,303 additional texts.
\end{itemize}

After combining and deduplicating, the raw pool contained 65,040 texts. X (Twitter) was excluded due to access-quota constraints that prevented building a usable pool. After cleaning and relevance filtering, the pool was reduced to \textbf{43,101 texts}, and a second pass produced \textbf{4,181 usable candidates}.

Using the same ensemble-based uncertainty sampling, 4,084 texts were selected for annotation in Label Studio with the same two-label scheme. Of these, 690 were labeled Christianity-related and 102 as hate speech (hate ratio: 2.5\%, somewhat lower than Chinese). A LUKE-based Japanese classifier \citep{luke-japanese} was fine-tuned on these labels using Focal Loss and back-translation augmentation for the minority class, then applied to the 43,101-text pool. Texts predicted as both Christianity-related and hateful formed the \textbf{native Japanese corpus of 1,445 texts} (1,610 predicted hateful; 6,491 predicted Christianity-related). Figure~\ref{fig:ja-funnel} shows the corresponding data funnel.

\subsection{Stage 2: LLM-Based Augmentation}
\label{subsec:ja-aug}

LLM-based localization for Japanese followed the same seed-based procedure. The generation prompt was adapted to target register conventions common in Japanese online forums, including the informal, anonymous posting style of 5ch \citep{cross-lingual-llm-localization, llm-style-transfer}. Post-generation filtering with the fine-tuned Japanese classifier processed 1,249 texts: 1,167 (93.4\%) passed Christianity-relevance and 945 (75.7\%) passed the hate speech filter. After requiring both, \textbf{915 synthetic texts} were retained.

The native and synthetic sets were merged to form the \textbf{final Japanese corpus of 2,360 texts}.

\subsection{Lexical Profile}

To characterise the topical focus of each corpus, we extracted high-frequency content terms after language-specific tokenisation (jieba for Chinese, janome for Japanese, TweetTokenizer for English), filtering out function words, particles, and punctuation. Table~\ref{tab:top-keywords} lists the ten most frequent terms per language.

\centering
\small

\toprule
Rank & Chinese & Japanese & English \\
\midrule
1  & God            & Unification Church & church    \\
2  & Jesus          & Christ             & catholic  \\
3  & Christianity   & church             & god       \\
4  & Bible          & religion           & jesus     \\
5  & Christian      & believer           & christian \\

\caption{Ten most frequent content terms per language (function words excluded; Chinese and Japanese terms shown as English translations). Counts based on the native corpora only (ZH: 1,070; JA: 1,445; EN: 58,940).}
\label{tab:top-keywords}
\end{table}

The Chinese corpus is anchored by core religious referents (God, Jesus, Christianity, Bible) alongside institutional terms such as \textit{church} and \textit{priest}. The high frequency of \textit{China} suggests a framing that ties Christianity to foreign-influence or national identity narratives.

The Japanese corpus is most distinctively shaped by \textit{Unification Church} (\textit{T\={o}itsu Ky\={o}kai}), reflecting the political salience of that organisation following the 2022 assassination of former Prime Minister Abe --- which appears directly as the sixth most frequent term. This indicates that Japanese anti-Christian discourse in the corpus is heavily intertwined with a specific domestic political event rather than theological opposition. Broader Christian framing is nonetheless present: Christ, church, religion, believer, Bible.

The English corpus clusters around institutional Catholicism (church, catholic, pope, catholics, bishops), consistent with the composition of the source corpora, where clergy abuse scandals and Catholic institutional authority are prominent themes.
